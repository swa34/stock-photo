Storing your photos & selling them online

Three honest ways to turn a large photo archive into a stock storefront — and what each one really costs, stores, and asks of you.

01 · Start here

The one idea that makes everything else simple

Your photos live two separate lives. Keeping them separate is what keeps storage cheap and the website fast.

The most common point of confusion is thinking the website and the archive are the same pile of files. They are not, and they should never be:

  • The archive is your private master collection — the big RAW files straight off the camera (roughly 25–60 MB each). Nobody buys these. They just need to exist forever, safely, and cheaply.
  • The storefront is the public website. It only ever shows high-resolution JPEGs (roughly 2–15 MB each) exported from Lightroom. That is the industry norm — stock buyers receive JPEGs, never RAW.

Because the two are decoupled, you can clear the heavy RAW files off your laptop without ever breaking the live site. The website never needed them.

Object storage — the part to understand for cost

This is where the master archive should live, and where the real money decisions are. The headline price is per terabyte, but the hidden cost is egress — the fee to download your own files back out. It is the single biggest trap in cloud storage.

Plain-English takeaway

For most photographers the answer is Backblaze B2 (cheap, generous free downloads) or Cloudflare R2 if you’ll pull files often (downloads are always free). 03 · Don't lose the originals

The 3-2-1 rule

A storefront is replaceable. The original photographs are not. The 3-2-1 rule — coined by photographer Peter Krogh in The DAM Book — is the standard insurance policy.

Watch out

A “backup” service like Backblaze Personal ($99/yr, unlimited) mirrors your computer — but it purges files from external drives you don’t reconnect within ~30 days. For a true permanent archive, pair it with object storage (Section 02), which never forgets.

04 · The storefront

Three ways to actually sell the photos

Every storefront has to do the same three things: show the gallery, let people search, and take payment then deliver the file. The options differ in how much is bundled for you versus assembled by you — which is the trade between cost, control, and effort.

When custom is worth it

Only choose this if Options A and B genuinely can’t do what you need — you’d be building and maintaining real software. For most photographers, WordPress (Option B) gives most of the control for a fraction of the effort.

06 · A nudge toward a choice

Which one is probably right

Not advice to follow blindly — just the most common fit for each situation, to give your own research a starting point.

If you want…
To start selling now

…and never think about servers, pick a managed platform (Option A). SmugMug or PhotoShelter for unlimited JPEG storage and built-in checkout.

≈ $10–45 / month, all-in
If you want…
Lowest cost at scale

…and you’re comfortable maintaining a site, self-host WordPress (Option B) with Easy Digital Downloads and images offloaded to Backblaze B2.

≈ $10–20 / month + $7 per TB
If you want…
A one-of-a-kind site

…that no existing platform can produce — and you have a developer on hand — build it totally custom (Option C) in Python or Node. Most control, most work.

≈ $15–60 / month infra + build time
In every case

The storefront is separate from the archive. Whichever path you choose, keep the RAW masters in object storage under the 3-2-1 rule (Sections 02–03). The shop can be rebuilt; the originals cannot.

07 · Plain definitions

Glossary

Object storage
Cloud storage priced by the terabyte, built to hold huge numbers of files cheaply. No “synced folder” — you push/pull with an app.
Egress
The fee some providers charge to download your own files back out. The sneaky part of a cloud bill.
RAW vs JPEG
RAW = the camera’s big original negative. JPEG = the compressed export buyers actually receive.
3-2-1 rule
3 copies, on 2 media types, 1 kept off-site. The standard backup insurance for photographers.
Cloud server (VPS / PaaS)
A computer you rent in a data center to run a custom app, billed monthly (~$5–25). “PaaS” is a friendlier, more managed version of the same idea.
Managed database
A hosted database (usually PostgreSQL) a custom site uses to track its photos, prices, and orders.
Payment processor
A service like Stripe or PayPal that takes the card payment — roughly 2.9% + 30¢ per sale.
IPTC metadata
The caption/keyword tags embedded inside a photo. Storefront search engines read these to make images findable.
Media offloading
Automatically moving a website’s big image files to cheap object storage so the site itself stays small and fast.
